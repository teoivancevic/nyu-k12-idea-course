## First: Form Your Teams

Yesterday was exploratory — you worked through the domain material individually and got a feel for the tools. Today is the first time you're working as an actual team.

Take 5 minutes to do this properly:

- Agree on who's on your team
- Agree on which domain you're working in
- Open a shared copy of the Day 1 worksheet — one doc, all three of you editing it together

Everything from here runs as a team.

---

## Pick Up Where You Left Off

Don't start over. Open your worksheet and find the furthest point you got to yesterday. That's where you start.

If you don't have a one-sentence problem statement yet, that's the only goal right now. Work through the prompt sequence for your domain on the [Day 1 workshop page](/day/1) — Steps 1 through 4 — until you have something specific enough to write down.

If you have a sentence but it feels vague, try this:

```
Here is our current problem statement: [paste your sentence]. Is this specific enough that two different teams working on the same domain would write different sentences? If not, what's the vaguest part, and what would make it more precise?
```

If you need a second data point to strengthen it:

```
I already have this data point: [your fact + source]. I'm working on [your domain]. What's a second angle I haven't measured yet that would strengthen the case that this problem is real and widespread? Point me to a specific dataset or report.
```

Don't move on until the sentence is done. It's the foundation for everything the professor covers this afternoon.

## Tomorrow's Speaker: Build Your Questions

Tomorrow's AM session includes a field interview with an industry guest. Before the end of today, your team needs at least two questions ready.

Good questions here are not general. They're specific to the gap your problem statement identified — the thing you still don't know that this person is most likely to have a real answer to.

Use this to get started:

```
Here is our problem statement: [paste it]. We're interviewing someone with experience in [civic tech / government / nonprofit — pick what fits]. What's the one thing we still don't know that this person is most likely to have a real answer to? Turn that into two specific interview questions we could ask in a 20-minute conversation.
```

Then refine from there. A good question produces a specific answer, not a general opinion.

## Class Question Brainstorm

*Last 20–30 minutes of the morning session.*

Each team shares their two questions. As a class we go through the full list together:

- **Cut** anything too broad to get a useful answer
- **Sharpen** the strongest ones so they're specific enough to actually use

Goal: walk into tomorrow's interview with 5–6 strong questions, not 20 vague ones.
